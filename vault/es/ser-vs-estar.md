# ser vs estar

ser vs estar::ser = essence/identity/origin; estar = state/location/condition #sr/es

## The split

- **ser** — permanent-ish: who/what something *is*. Profession, nationality, time, possession, material.
  - *Soy belga.* — I'm Belgian.
  - *Son las tres.* — It's three o'clock.
- **estar** — temporary-ish: how/where something *is right now*. Location, mood, ongoing action.
  - *Estoy cansado.* — I'm tired.
  - *Está en Madrid.* — He's in Madrid.

## The tricky cases

Same adjective, different verb, different meaning:
- *Es aburrido.* — He is boring. (personality)
- *Está aburrido.* — He is bored. (state)
- *Es listo.* — He is clever.
- *Está listo.* — He is ready.

## Notes

The "permanent vs temporary" rule is a lie that works 80% of the time. The real rule is *essence vs state*. Location is always *estar* even though a mountain isn't moving.

## French bridge

**French collapses both into *être*.** This is the single biggest reason Spanish feels harder than French if you come from French: you suddenly have to make a distinction that French never forced you to think about.

- *Je suis belge* → *Soy belga* (essence → ser)
- *Je suis fatigué* → *Estoy cansado* (state → estar)
- *Je suis à Madrid* → *Estoy en Madrid* (location → estar, despite "I AM Belgian" using ser)
